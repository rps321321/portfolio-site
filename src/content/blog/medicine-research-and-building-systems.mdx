# Medicine, Research, and Building Technical Systems

I am a second-year medical student, but a big part of my work also lives in software.

That combination is not accidental. Medicine trains you to be methodical: define the problem clearly, gather evidence carefully, question assumptions, and avoid making confident claims without support. You are often asked to work from incomplete information without losing discipline. That way of thinking carries directly into how I build technical systems.

## What That Looks Like In Practice

Much of my software work sits around Python, scraping, AI tooling, and structured data workflows.

I have built projects that:

- collect public data from dynamic websites using browser automation
- normalize messy outputs into consistent formats
- store results in structured systems such as SQLite
- export datasets for analysis and review
- support downstream research and evaluation work

I am especially interested in projects where technical work is not just about shipping code, but about making information more usable, more reliable, and easier to evaluate. The habits that carry over most clearly from medicine are structured observation, careful documentation, consistency in evaluation, and respect for evidence over assumption.

## Research Work

One of the clearest examples of that intersection is a university research project I co-authored at the Faculty of Medicine, University of Nis.

The paper examined TikTok content as a source of education in medical statistics. As part of that work, a Python-based data collection workflow was used to gather public video information for analysis. The study then evaluated the quality and reliability of the selected content using structured scoring frameworks.

That project was important to me because it made something very clear: software can be part of research methodology, not just a separate hobby beside it. Instead of talking generally about educational content online, the work depended on a real dataset collected through a reproducible technical process.

That project mattered to me because it connected several things I care about at once:

- medical education
- information quality
- public-facing online platforms
- reproducible data collection
- critical evaluation instead of passive consumption

## How I Think About Building

I like building systems that are useful, inspectable, and grounded in real constraints.

That includes:

- scraping workflows that deal with dynamic pages instead of idealized examples
- AI tooling that helps with real tasks instead of just demos
- interfaces that make technical systems easier to understand
- research-oriented workflows where data quality actually matters

In both medicine and software, access to information is not the same as confidence in it. A system can be fast, convenient, and impressive on the surface, but that does not automatically make it dependable. I care most about building workflows where the output is trustworthy enough to actually use.

I am still early in both medicine and software, but I have already had the chance to work in a way that combines both. That combination is what I want this portfolio to represent more honestly: not just concepts, but practical work shaped by scientific training and technical curiosity.
